3. The Three Forms of a Quadratic MCAP Essential

Tier 1 — What Each Form Reveals

  • Standard Form: \( y = ax^2 + bx + c \)
    Reveals the \( y \)-intercept (\( c \)). Opens up if \( a > 0 \), down if \( a < 0 \).
  • Vertex Form: \( y = a(x - h)^2 + k \)
    Reveals the vertex directly at \( (h, k) \). Best for graphing quickly.
  • Factored (Intercept) Form: \( y = a(x - r_1)(x - r_2) \)
    Reveals the \( x \)-intercepts (zeros or roots) at \( x = r_1 \) and \( x = r_2 \).

Tier 2 — Converting Between Forms

To move between forms, apply algebraic operations:

  • Standard → Factored: Factor the trinomial.
  • Standard → Vertex: Complete the square, OR use \( x = \frac{-b}{2a} \) to find \( h \), then plug in to find \( k \).
  • Vertex/Factored → Standard: Expand (foil/box) and distribute \( a \).

4. Solving by Factoring MCAP Essential

Tier 1 — The Zero Product Property

If \( A \times B = 0 \), then either \( A = 0 \) or \( B = 0 \). This is the absolute foundation of solving by factoring.

  1. Move everything to one side so the equation equals zero: \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \).
  2. Factor the polynomial completely. Always check for a GCF first!
  3. Set each factor equal to zero and solve.
Example: Simple Factoring (\( a = 1 \))

Solve: \( x^2 - 7x = -10 \)

1. Set to zero: \( x^2 - 7x + 10 = 0 \)

2. Factor: What adds to -7 and multiplies to 10? (-5 and -2)

3. Rewrite: \( (x - 5)(x - 2) = 0 \)

4. Solve: \( x - 5 = 0 \implies x = 5 \), and \( x - 2 = 0 \implies x = 2 \)

Roots: \( \{2, 5\} \)

Example: Harder Factoring (\( a \neq 1 \)) — The AC/Grouping Method

Solve: \( 6x^2 + 7x - 3 = 0 \)

1. Multiply \( a \times c \): \( 6 \times (-3) = -18 \). Now find two numbers that multiply to -18 and add to +7 (the middle coefficient).

2. Those numbers are \( +9 \) and \( -2 \) because \( 9 \times (-2) = -18 \) and \( 9 + (-2) = 7 \).

3. Split the middle term: \( 6x^2 + 9x - 2x - 3 = 0 \)

4. Group into two pairs: \( (6x^2 + 9x) + (-2x - 3) = 0 \)

5. Factor each group: \( 3x(2x + 3) - 1(2x + 3) = 0 \)

6. Factor out the common binomial: \( (3x - 1)(2x + 3) = 0 \)

7. Solve: \( x = \frac{1}{3} \) or \( x = -\frac{3}{2} \)

5. The Quadratic Formula MCAP Essential

Tier 1 — The Formula

When factoring gets messy (especially when \( a \neq 1 \) or roots are irrational/complex), use the formula.

\( x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \)
Example: Solve \( 3x^2 + 2x - 5 = 0 \)

Here \( a = 3, b = 2, c = -5 \).

Discriminant: \( (2)^2 - 4(3)(-5) = 4 + 60 = 64 \). Positive, so 2 real roots.

\( x = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{64}}{2(3)} = \frac{-2 \pm 8}{6} \)

\( x = \frac{-2 + 8}{6} = 1 \) or \( x = \frac{-2 - 8}{6} = \frac{-10}{6} = -\frac{5}{3} \)

Roots: \( \{1, -\frac{5}{3}\} \)

Tier 3 — Tutor Notes: Derivation context

Don't let them just memorize it blindly. Remind Tracy that the formula is literally just the result of performing "Completing the Square" on the generic equation \( ax^2 + bx + c = 0 \). It is a shortcut algorithm.

6. Completing the Square Classroom/MCAP

This is the most structurally important algebraic manipulation in Algebra 2. It forces a perfect square trinomial.

Why Does "Half-and-Square" Work? (The Intuition)

Think of it like building a square out of blocks. If you have \( x^2 + 6x \), imagine a square with side \( x \) (area \( x^2 \)) and a rectangle with dimensions \( x \times 6 \) (area \( 6x \)).

Split that \( 6x \) rectangle in half: two strips of \( x \times 3 \). Attach one strip to the right of the square and one to the bottom. You now have an almost-complete bigger square with side \( (x + 3) \) — but there's a little \( 3 \times 3 = 9 \) gap in the bottom-right corner.

"Completing the square" literally means adding that missing 9 to fill the gap! That's WHY we take half of 6 (= 3) and square it (= 9). We're building a geometric square.

Tier 2 — The Algorithm

To solve \( x^2 + 6x - 7 = 0 \):

  1. Move constant to the right: \( x^2 + 6x = 7 \)
  2. Take half of \( b \) (which is 6), square it, and add to BOTH sides: \( (\frac{6}{2})^2 = 9 \).
    \( x^2 + 6x + 9 = 7 + 9 \)
  3. Factor the left side as a perfect square: \( (x + 3)^2 = 16 \)
  4. Take the square root of both sides (don't forget \( \pm \) !): \( x + 3 = \pm 4 \)
  5. Solve for \( x \): \( x = -3 \pm 4 \), so \( x = 1 \) or \( x = -7 \).

Note: If \( a \neq 1 \), factor \( a \) out of the \( x^2 \) and \( x \) terms first!

⚠️ Common D-Student Mistake

Wrong: Adding 9 to the LEFT side but forgetting to add 9 to the RIGHT side.

If you add something to one side of an equation and not the other, the equation is no longer balanced. It's mathematically illegal! Whatever you do to one side, you MUST do to the other.

7. The Discriminant & Nature of Solutions MCAP Essential

The discriminant is the piece under the square root: \( \Delta = b^2 - 4ac \). It tells you how many and what type of roots exist without solving the whole equation.

Discriminant (\( \Delta \)) Roots (Solutions) Graph (x-intercepts)
Positive (\( \Delta > 0 \)) 2 Real Roots Crosses x-axis twice
Zero (\( \Delta = 0 \)) 1 Real Root (Repeated / Multiplicity 2) Vertex touches x-axis exactly once
Negative (\( \Delta < 0 \)) 2 Complex Roots (Conjugate pair) Does not cross the x-axis

8. Graph Connections & Systems MCAP Essential

Tier 2 — Linear-Quadratic Systems

Where does a line intersect a parabola? A system can have 0, 1, or 2 intersection points.

The Strategy: substitution.

  1. Set the line equation equal to the parabola equation: \( mx + d = ax^2 + bx + c \)
  2. Move everything to one side to form a NEW quadratic: \( ax^2 + (b-m)x + (c-d) = 0 \)
  3. Solve this new quadratic (by factoring or formula). The \( x \)-values are the intersection coordinates.
  4. Plug \( x \) back into the linear equation to find the \( y \)-coordinates.
Example: Solve the system \( y = x^2 - 2x \) and \( y = x + 4 \)

1. Set equal: \( x + 4 = x^2 - 2x \)

2. Rearrange: \( 0 = x^2 - 3x - 4 \)

3. Factor: \( 0 = (x - 4)(x + 1) \), so \( x = 4 \) or \( x = -1 \)

4. Plug back into the line (easier equation):
\( x = 4 \implies y = 4 + 4 = 8 \)
\( x = -1 \implies y = -1 + 4 = 3 \)

Intersection points: \( (4, 8) \) and \( (-1, 3) \)

9. Quadratic Transformations MCAP Essential

Looking at vertex form: \( y = a(x - h)^2 + k \)

  • \( a \) (Vertical Stretch/Compression): If \( |a| > 1 \), the parabola is stretched (narrower). If \( 0 < |a| < 1 \), it is compressed (wider). If \( a \) is negative, it reflects across the x-axis.
  • \( h \) (Horizontal Shift): Shifts exactly \( h \) units. Watch the sign! \( (x - 3)^2 \) moves RIGHT 3. \( (x + 4)^2 \) moves LEFT 4.
  • \( k \) (Vertical Shift): Shifts exactly \( k \) units up or down.

10. Formative Check & Practice

Practice:

  1. Solve by factoring: \( 2x^2 - 11x + 12 = 0 \)
  2. Solve using completing the square: \( x^2 - 8x + 5 = 0 \)
  3. Find the discriminant and state the number of real roots: \( -3x^2 + 6x - 4 = 0 \)
  4. Write the quadratic \( y = x^2 + 10x + 24 \) in vertex form. Determine the vertex and transformations applied to the parent function \( y = x^2 \).
Solutions (Expand to check)

1. Factoring: \( (2x - 3)(x - 4) = 0 \implies x = \frac{3}{2}, x = 4 \)

2. Completing Square: \( x^2 - 8x + 16 = -5 + 16 \implies (x-4)^2 = 11 \implies x = 4 \pm \sqrt{11} \)

3. Discriminant: \( b^2 - 4ac = (6)^2 - 4(-3)(-4) = 36 - 48 = -12 \). Because it is negative, there are 0 real roots (it has 2 complex roots).

4. Vertex Form: \( y = (x^2 + 10x + 25) + 24 - 25 \implies y = (x + 5)^2 - 1 \). Vertex is \( (-5, -1) \). Shifted left 5, down 1.
