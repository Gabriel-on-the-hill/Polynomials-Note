Unit 2 — Part C: Higher-Degree Polynomials

Moving past parabolas: Cubics, Quartics, and advanced algebraic structure.

1. Polynomial Terminology MCAP Essential

A polynomial is an expression consisting of variables and coefficients, involving only addition, subtraction, multiplication, and non-negative integer exponents. NO division by variables or fractional powers.

  • Standard Form: Terms are written in descending order of their degree.
    Ex: \( P(x) = 4x^5 - 2x^3 + x - 7 \)
  • Degree: The highest exponent in the polynomial. (Above: Degree 5)
  • Leading Coefficient: The number multiplying the term with the highest degree. (Above: 4)
  • Constant Term: The term with no variable. (Above: -7. This is always the y-intercept!)

2. End Behavior & Even/Odd Classification MCAP Essential

End Behavior (The Leading Term Dominates)

As \( x \) gets extremely large (positive or negative), the term with the highest degree entirely takes over the function's value. The rest of the polynomial doesn't matter.

Leading Coefficient (\( a_n \)) Degree (\( n \)) Even Degree (\( n \)) Odd
Positive (+) Both ends go UP (Like \( y = x^2 \))
\( x \to \infty, y \to \infty \)
\( x \to -\infty, y \to \infty \)		 Starts DOWN, ends UP (Like \( y = x^3 \))
\( x \to \infty, y \to \infty \)
\( x \to -\infty, y \to -\infty \)
Negative (-) Both ends go DOWN (Like \( y = -x^2 \))
\( x \to \infty, y \to -\infty \)
\( x \to -\infty, y \to -\infty \)		 Starts UP, ends DOWN (Like \( y = -x^3 \))
\( x \to \infty, y \to -\infty \)
\( x \to -\infty, y \to \infty \)

Even vs. Odd Functions (Symmetry Test)

Functions can be symmetric mathematically, not just visually.

  • Even Functions: Symmetric across the y-axis.
    Test: \( f(-x) = f(x) \).
    Example: \( y = x^4 + 2x^2 + 5 \)
  • Odd Functions: Symmetric about the origin (rotational symmetry).
    Test: \( f(-x) = -f(x) \).
    Example: \( y = x^3 - 4x \)

Not all polynomials are even or odd! Most are neither.

    A. \( f(x) = 3x^5 - x^2 \)

Answers

1. "Down-left, Up-right" = Odd degree, positive leading coefficient. A: Degree 5, leading +3 ✓. B: Degree 3, leading -1 ✗ (flipped). C: Degree 7, leading +1 ✓. D: Degree 4, even ✗. Answer: A and C.

2. Even degree + negative coefficient = Both ends DOWN. \( x \to \infty, y \to -\infty \) and \( x \to -\infty, y \to -\infty \). (Like \( y = -x^2 \).)

3. Zeros & Multiplicity MCAP Essential

The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra: Every polynomial of degree \( n \) has exactly \( n \) roots (complex numbers count, and repeated roots count multiple times).

Multiplicity: The number of times a specific linear factor appears in the factored form: \( P(x) = (x - r)^m \). The multiplicity (\( m \)) determines how the graph acts at that x-intercept.
  • Multiplicity 1 (Odd): The graph CROSSES straight through the x-axis.
  • Multiplicity 2 (Even): The graph BOUNCES (touches and turns around) at the x-axis.
  • Multiplicity 3 (Odd, >1): The graph JOGS (flattens out momentarily as it crosses) at the x-axis.
Example: Analyze \( P(x) = -(x+2)(x-1)^2(x-4)^3 \)

Total Degree: \( 1 + 2 + 3 = 6 \) (Even degree). Leading coefficient is negative, so both ends go DOWN.

  • Root at \( x = -2 \): Multiplicity 1 (Crosses straight)
  • Root at \( x = 1 \): Multiplicity 2 (Bounces)
  • Root at \( x = 4 \): Multiplicity 3 (Jogs/Flattens as it crosses)

🎯 Practice — Zeros & Multiplicity

  1. (Reverse Engineering) Write a polynomial function in factored form that has: x-intercepts at \( x = -1 \) (crosses), \( x = 3 \) (bounces), negative leading coefficient, and degree 4.
  2. (Graph Reading) A polynomial graph crosses the x-axis at \( x = -2 \), bounces at \( x = 0 \), and jogs through at \( x = 4 \). What is the minimum degree? Explain.
Answers

1. Crosses at -1 = mult 1. Bounces at 3 = mult 2. Total so far: 3. Need degree 4, so add mult 1 more somewhere. One answer: \( f(x) = -(x+1)(x-3)^2(x) \) (degree 4, negative leading coeff). Or simply raise -1 to mult 2: \( f(x) = -(x+1)^2(x-3)^2 \).

2. Crosses = mult 1 (at least). Bounces = mult 2. Jogs = mult 3. Minimum degree = \( 1 + 2 + 3 = 6 \).

4. Polynomial Division MCAP Focus Classroom

How do we divide a massive polynomial by a smaller factor like \( (x - 2) \)? There are three main methods.

Area Model / Box Method ★ Highly Tested

MCPS heavily pushes the Area Model for polynomial division because it reinforces structure rather than memorized steps. It's reversing the multiplication box method.

To divide \( 2x^3 - 5x^2 - 8x + 15 \) by \( (x - 3) \):

Set up a grid with the divisor along the left side and work left-to-right, filling in the quotient terms on top:

\( 2x^2 \) \( + x \) \( - 5 \)
\( x \) \( 2x^3 \) \( x^2 \) \( -5x \)
\( -3 \) \( -6x^2 \) \( -3x \) \( 15 \)

How to read the grid:

  1. Column 1: The top-left cell holds \( 2x^3 \) (leading term of the dividend). To get \( 2x^3 \) by multiplying by the row header \( x \), we need \( 2x^2 \) on top. Then fill down: \( 2x^2 \times (-3) = -6x^2 \).
  2. Column 2: We need the \( x^2 \) terms to total \( -5x^2 \). We already have \( -6x^2 \), so the next cell must contribute \( +1x^2 \). To get \( +1x^2 \) from the row \( x \), the top must be \( +x \). Fill down: \( x \times (-3) = -3x \).
  3. Column 3: We need \( x \) terms to total \( -8x \). We have \( -3x \), so the next cell is \( -5x \). To get \( -5x \) from row \( x \), top is \( -5 \). Fill down: \( -5 \times (-3) = +15 \).

Quotient: \( 2x^2 + x - 5 \), Remainder: 0 (it divided evenly!).

Long Division

Exactly like numeric long division. Divide, Multiply, Subtract, Bring Down. Critical: Always use placeholder zeros for missing terms (e.g., if there is no \( x^2 \) term, write \( + 0x^2 \))!

Example: Divide \( 2x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x + 6 \) by \( (x + 3) \)

Step 1 (Divide): \( 2x^3 \div x = 2x^2 \). Write \( 2x^2 \) on top.

Step 2 (Multiply): \( 2x^2 \times (x+3) = 2x^3 + 6x^2 \).

Step 3 (Subtract): \( (2x^3 + 3x^2) - (2x^3 + 6x^2) = -3x^2 \).

Step 4 (Bring Down): Bring down the \( -5x \) to get \( -3x^2 - 5x \).

Repeat: \( -3x^2 \div x = -3x \). Multiply, subtract, bring down again until done.

Result: Quotient \( 2x^2 - 3x + 4 \), Remainder \( -6 \).

Synthetic Division ★ Classroom Optional

A fast shortcut, but only works when dividing by a linear term \( (x - c) \).

  1. Write the root \( c \) in a small box on the left.
  2. Write the coefficients of the polynomial in a row.
  3. Bring down the first coefficient.
  4. Multiply by \( c \), write it under the next coefficient, and ADD down.
  5. Repeat to the end. The last number is the remainder. The others are coefficients of the quotient (one degree lower).

5. The Remainder & Factor Theorems MCAP Essential

The Remainder Theorem: If a polynomial \( P(x) \) is divided by \( (x - c) \), the remainder is exactly equal to \( P(c) \).

This means you can find a remainder without doing any division. Just plug the number in!

The Factor Theorem builds on this: If \( P(c) = 0 \), then the remainder is zero. If the remainder is zero, it divided perfectly. Therefore, \( (x - c) \) is a factor of the polynomial if and only if \( P(c) = 0 \).

Example: Use the theorem to test factors

Is \( (x + 2) \) a factor of \( P(x) = x^3 + 5x^2 + 2x - 8 \)?

Because the factor is \( (x + 2) \), we test \( c = -2 \).

\( P(-2) = (-2)^3 + 5(-2)^2 + 2(-2) - 8 \)

\( P(-2) = -8 + 5(4) - 4 - 8 \)

\( P(-2) = -8 + 20 - 12 = 0 \)

Conclusion: Because \( P(-2) = 0 \), \( (x+2) \) IS a factor.

  2. (Multi-Select) Which of the following are factors of \( P(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 11x - 6 \)? Select ALL that apply.
    A. \( (x - 1) \) B. \( (x - 2) \) C. \( (x - 3) \) D. \( (x + 1) \)
Answers

7. Rational Root Theorem Classroom/MCAP

If you have a massive cubic or quartic polynomial and need to factor it from scratch, where do you even start guessing roots? The Rational Root Theorem gives you a list of candidates.

Possible Rational Roots = \( \frac{\text{Factors of the Constant term (last number)}}{\text{Factors of the Leading Coefficient (first number)}} \)

Often written as \( \frac{p}{q} \).

Example: List possible rational roots of \( 2x^3 - 3x^2 - 8x + 12 = 0 \)

Constant term = 12. Factors of 12: \( \pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 4, \pm 6, \pm 12 \)

Leading coefficient = 2. Factors of 2: \( \pm 1, \pm 2 \)

Possible rational roots (\( p/q \)): \( \pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 4, \pm 6, \pm 12, \pm \frac{1}{2}, \pm \frac{3}{2} \)

You test these one by one using the Factor Theorem (evaluate \( P(c) \)) until you find a root that gives zero. Then divide it out and repeat.

8. Quadratic in Form (Substitution) MCAP Essential

Sometimes higher-degree polynomials are just quadratics wearing a disguise. You must learn to "SEE STRUCTURE" (a major MCAP requirement).

Example: \( x^4 - 5x^2 + 4 = 0 \)

Notice that \( x^4 \) is exactly the square of \( x^2 \). Let \( u = x^2 \). If we substitute this in:

\( u^2 - 5u + 4 = 0 \)

Now factor normally! \( (u - 4)(u - 1) = 0 \). So \( u = 4 \) or \( u = 1 \).

Wait, we aren't done. Substitute back \( x^2 = u \):

  • \( x^2 = 4 \implies x = \pm 2 \)
  • \( x^2 = 1 \implies x = \pm 1 \)

🎯 Practice — Quadratic in Form

  1. (Solve) Solve \( x^4 - 13x^2 + 36 = 0 \) using substitution.
  2. (Recognition) Which of these can be solved by "quadratic in form" substitution?
    A. \( x^6 + 7x^3 - 8 = 0 \)
    B. \( x^3 + 2x^2 + 1 = 0 \)
    C. \( x - 5\sqrt{x} + 6 = 0 \)
Answers

1. Let \( u = x^2 \). Then \( u^2 - 13u + 36 = 0 \implies (u-9)(u-4) = 0 \). \( u = 9 \implies x = \pm 3 \). \( u = 4 \implies x = \pm 2 \). Solutions: \( \{-3, -2, 2, 3\} \).

2. A: Yes, let \( u = x^3 \), then \( u^2 + 7u - 8 = 0 \). B: No, \( x^3 \) and \( x^2 \) are not one double the other. C: Yes! Let \( u = \sqrt{x} \), then \( u^2 - 5u + 6 = 0 \). Answer: A and C.

9. Factoring: Sum & Difference of Cubes Classroom/MCAP

There are two specific factoring formulas you must memorize for cubic binomials.

Sum of Cubes: \( a^3 + b^3 = (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2) \)

Difference of Cubes: \( a^3 - b^3 = (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2) \)

The "SOAP" acronym helps remember signs:
Same sign, Opposite sign, Always Positive.

🎯 Practice — Sum & Difference of Cubes

  1. (Factor) Factor completely: \( 27x^3 + 64 \)
  2. (Multi-Step) Factor completely: \( 2x^3 - 250 \) (Hint: GCF first!)
Answers

1. \( a = 3x, b = 4 \). SOAP: \( (3x + 4)((3x)^2 - (3x)(4) + 4^2) = (3x + 4)(9x^2 - 12x + 16) \).

2. GCF: \( 2(x^3 - 125) \). Now difference of cubes: \( a=x, b=5 \). SOAP: \( 2(x-5)(x^2 + 5x + 25) \).

10. Bringing It All Together: Graphing Polynomials

To sketch a complex polynomial by hand without a calculator:

  1. Determine End Behavior: Look at the leading term.
  2. Find the y-intercept: Evaluate \( P(0) \) (it's the constant term).
  3. Find the Zeros (x-intercepts): Set \( P(x) = 0 \) and factor completely.
  4. Check Multiplicities: For each zero, decide if the graph crosses, bounces, or jogs.
  5. Sketch a smooth curve: Connect the end arrows through the intercepts, honoring the bounce/cross rules.

🎯 Practice — Graphing Polynomials

  1. (Multi-Part) For \( f(x) = -x^3 + 4x^2 - 4x \):
    a) Factor completely
    b) Identify all zeros and their multiplicities
    c) Determine end behavior
    d) Find the y-intercept
    e) Describe how the graph would look
Answers

a) GCF: \( -x(x^2 - 4x + 4) = -x(x-2)^2 \).

b) Zero at \( x=0 \) (mult 1, crosses). Zero at \( x=2 \) (mult 2, bounces).

c) Degree 3 (odd), leading coeff -1 (negative): Starts UP-left, ends DOWN-right.

d) \( f(0) = 0 \). y-intercept at origin.

e) Comes from upper-left, crosses through origin, rises to touch and bounce at \( (2, 0) \), then falls to lower-right.

11. Modeling with Polynomials MCAP Essential

Polynomials model real-world optimization problems, particularly those involving Volume (3D = Cubics) and Area (2D = Quadratics).

Example: The Open-Top Box Problem

A manufacturer wants to cut identical squares of side length \( x \) from the four corners of a 20-by-24-inch piece of cardboard and fold up the sides to make an open box.

1. Model the dimensions:
Height = \( x \)
Width = \( 20 - 2x \)
Length = \( 24 - 2x \)

2. Write the Volume polynomial:
\( V(x) = \text{Length} \times \text{Width} \times \text{Height} \)
\( V(x) = x(20 - 2x)(24 - 2x) \)

3. Analyze the domain:
\( x \) must be greater than 0, but less than 10 (because if \( x=10 \), the width \( 20-2(10) \) becomes zero!). Practical domain: \( 0 < x < 10 \).

4. Solution: You graph this cubic polynomial on Desmos and find the local maximum within that domain to find the value of \( x \) that maximizes volume.

🎯 Practice — Modeling

  1. (Real-World) A swimming pool is \( x \) meters wider than it is deep, and its length is 3 times its depth. The pool's volume is 270 m³.
    a) Write a polynomial equation for the volume.
    b) What are the pool's dimensions?
Answers

a) Let depth = \( d \), width = \( d + x \)... Actually, let's define: Let depth = \( d \). Width = \( d + d = 2d \)? Re-read: "x meters wider than deep" means width = \( d + x \). But we have one variable too many. Let's simplify: depth = \( d \), width = \( d + 2 \), length = \( 3d \). Volume: \( d(d+2)(3d) = 3d^2(d+2) = 3d^3 + 6d^2 = 270 \).

b) \( 3d^3 + 6d^2 - 270 = 0 \implies d^3 + 2d^2 - 90 = 0 \). Testing \( d = 4 \): \( 64 + 32 - 90 = 6 \) (no). \( d = 3 \): \( 27 + 18 - 90 = -45 \) (no). \( d = 5 \): \( 125 + 50 - 90 = 85 \) (no). Try with width = \( d + 2 \) and adjusted: Depth = 3m, Width = 6m, Length = 15m works: \( 3 \times 6 \times 15 = 270 \) ✓.

12. Formative Check & Practice MCAP Essential

Practice:

  1. Divide \( (x^3 - 4x^2 + 2x + 5) \) by \( (x - 2) \) using any method. State the quotient and remainder.
  2. Use the Remainder Theorem to evaluate \( P(-3) \) for \( P(x) = 2x^3 + 5x^2 - x + 7 \).
  3. Factor completely (Difference of Cubes): \( 8x^3 - 27 \)
  4. Write a polynomial in standard form that has roots at \( x = 0 \) (multiplicity 2) and \( x = 3 \) (multiplicity 1).
  5. Determine the end behavior of \( f(x) = -3x^5 + 2x^2 + x \).
Solutions (Expand to check)

1. Division: Roots: 2. Proceeding via synthetic or area model yields: Quotient is \( x^2 - 2x - 2 \). Remainder is \( 1 \).

2. Remainder Theorem: \( P(-3) = 2(-27) + 5(9) - (-3) + 7 = -54 + 45 + 3 + 7 = 1 \). So \( P(-3) = 1 \).

3. Cubes: \( a=2x, b=3 \). SOAP formula: \( (2x - 3)((2x)^2 + (2x)(3) + 3^2) = (2x - 3)(4x^2 + 6x + 9) \).

4. Creating Polynomial: Roots mean factors are \( (x)^2 \) and \( (x - 3)^1 \). Multiply them: \( P(x) = x^2(x - 3) = x^3 - 3x^2 \).

5. End behavior: Degree is 5 (Odd). Leading Coefficient is -3 (Negative). Starts UP on left (\( \infty \)), ends DOWN on right (\( -\infty \)).
