Algebra 2 Excellence Bible — Part 4: Probability

Core principle: Probability = winners ÷ total (but the work is in enumerating correctly).

If you can list them, you can count them. If you can count them, you can reason.

Foundation F10 — Combinatorics (Counting)

Spiral link: Fractions  →  Probability = Winners / Total

Tier 1 — Permutation vs Combination (What changes?)

Ask first: Does order matter?

  • Permutation (order matters): arrangements like first/second/third, codes.
  • Combination (order doesn't matter): choosing a committee, a set of winners.

Quick intuition: nPr ≥ nCr. You remove duplicates (divide by r!) to get combinations.

Worked example — 3 letters from ABC

Permutations (order matters): ABC, ACB, BAC, BCA, CAB, CBA → 6 = 3!
Combinations (order doesn't matter): {A,B,C} → 1 (choose 3 of 3). If choose 2: AB, AC, BC → 3.

Tier 2 — Standard Formulas

Quantity Formula Comment
Permutation nPr = n! / (n - r)! Order matters
Combination nCr = n! / (r! (n - r)!) Order does not matter
Factorial n! = n × (n-1) × ... × 1 0! = 1 by convention
Interactive (Try it):
Uses multiplicative approach to avoid huge intermediate factorials.

Foundation F11 — Rules: AND, OR, Complement

Spiral link: Sets & Venn diagrams — overlap matters.

Tier 1 — Keywords & Quick Rules

AND → Multiply (for independent events).
OR → Add, but subtract overlap unless mutually exclusive.
Complement → P(A') = 1 − P(A).

Worked example — OR with overlap

In a deck, P(Heart) = 13/52 = 1/4. P(Ace) = 4/52 = 1/13. P(Heart OR Ace) = P(Heart)+P(Ace) − P(Heart AND Ace).

P(Heart AND Ace) = 1 (Ace of hearts) / 52 = 1/52. So total = 13/52 + 4/52 − 1/52 = 16/52 = 4/13.

Tier 2 — Mutually Exclusive

Mutually exclusive events cannot both occur. If they are, P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B). Otherwise use P(A or B) = P(A)+P(B) − P(A and B).

Foundation F12 — Conditional Probability & Independence

Spiral link: Tree diagrams — moving down a branch changes the sample space.

Tier 1 — The "Given" (P(A | B))

Definition: P(A | B) = P(A and B) / P(B) — change the denominator to the condition.

Independence check: A and B independent ⇔ P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B).

Worked example — cards without replacement

Probability first card is Ace and second (without replacement) is Ace:

P(first Ace) = 4/52. Given that, P(second Ace) = 3/51. So P(both) = (4/52)×(3/51) = 1/221.

Tier 2 — Bayes (Intro)

Bayes uses conditional probabilities to reverse conditioning. For Algebra 2, understand structure before formula: P(A|B) proportional to P(B|A)P(A).

Common Pitfalls & Quick Checks

Tier 3 — Pitfalls

  • Mixing up with/without replacement — always adjust counts when items are removed.
  • Using addition for OR without removing overlap.
  • For conditional: change the denominator to the condition (don’t forget to restrict the universe).
  • Permutation vs combination confusion — ask the "order?" question always.

Sample Exam-Style Question (with solution)

Q: From 6 men and 4 women, randomly choose 3 people. What is the probability the selection contains exactly 2 women?

Solution:

  1. Count winners: Choose 2 women from 4 → C(4,2) = 6. Choose 1 man from 6 → C(6,1) = 6. Total winners = 6×6 = 36.
  2. Total ways: choose any 3 from 10 → C(10,3) = 120.
  3. Probability = 36 / 120 = 3 / 10.
