Unit 2 — Part B: Complex Numbers

Expanding the number system to solve "impossible" equations.

1. Why Do We Need Them? Course Context

In Part A, we saw that sometimes a quadratic equation leaves a negative number under the square root (when the discriminant \( \Delta < 0 \)).

For example: \( x^2 = -9 \). In the real number system, this has no solution because any real number multiplied by itself is positive. But mathematicians realized that stopping at "no solution" broke many useful properties of algebra. We needed a new rule.

The Imaginary Unit: We define a special number, \( i \), such that \( i^2 = -1 \).

2. The Imaginary Unit \( i \) MCAP Essential

Core Definition: \( i = \sqrt{-1} \)

Tier 1 — The Cycle of Powers

Because \( i = \sqrt{-1} \), multiplying \( i \) by itself creates a predictable cycle of 4 values:

  • \( i^1 = i \)
  • \( i^2 = -1 \)
  • \( i^3 = -i \) because \( i^2 \times i = -1 \times i \)
  • \( i^4 = 1 \) because \( (-1) \times (-1) = 1 \)
  • \( i^5 = i \) (The cycle repeats!)

Trick: To evaluate large powers of \( i \) (like \( i^{35} \)), divide the exponent by 4 and look at the remainder. The remainder corresponds to the power in the cycle list above. \( 35 \div 4 = 8 \) remainder \( 3 \), so \( i^{35} = i^3 = -i \).

Tier 2 — Standard Form of a Complex Number

A complex number has a real part and an imaginary part. They cannot be combined into a single term.

Standard Form: \( a + bi \)

  • \( 3 + 2i \) (Real part: 3, Imaginary part: 2)
  • \( 5 \) (Real part: 5, Imaginary part: 0. All real numbers are complex numbers!)
  • \( -4i \) (Real part: 0, Imaginary part: -4. Purely imaginary number.)

🎯 Practice — The Imaginary Unit

  1. (Powers of i) Simplify: \( i^{42} \)
  2. (Simplify Radical) Simplify \( \sqrt{-72} \) completely.
Answers

1. \( 42 \div 4 = 10 \) remainder \( 2 \). So \( i^{42} = i^2 = -1 \).

2. \( \sqrt{-72} = \sqrt{72} \cdot \sqrt{-1} = \sqrt{36 \cdot 2} \cdot i = 6i\sqrt{2} \).

3. Complex Number Arithmetic MCAP Essential

Treat \( i \) exactly like the variable \( x \), but whenever you get an \( i^2 \), replace it with \( -1 \).

Addition & Subtraction

Combine like terms. Reals with reals, imaginaries with imaginaries.

Example: \( (4 - 2i) - (1 + 5i) \)

Distribute the negative: \( 4 - 2i - 1 - 5i \)

Combine Reals: \( 4 - 1 = 3 \)

Combine Imaginaries: \( -2i - 5i = -7i \)

Answer: \( 3 - 7i \)

Multiplication

Use FOIL/Box method, then replace \( i^2 \) with \(-1\).

Example: \( (2 + 3i)(4 - i) \)

FOIL: \( (2\times4) + (2\times-i) + (3i\times4) + (3i\times-i) \)

Simplify: \( 8 - 2i + 12i - 3i^2 \)

Combine: \( 8 + 10i - 3(-1) \)

Replace \( i^2 \): \( 8 + 10i + 3 \)

Answer: \( 11 + 10i \)

Division & Complex Conjugates

You cannot leave an \( i \) in the denominator. Multiply the numerator and denominator by the complex conjugate of the denominator.

The conjugate of \( (a + bi) \) is \( (a - bi) \). When you multiply them, the \( i \) terms cancel out entirely: \( (a+bi)(a-bi) = a^2 - b^2i^2 = a^2 + b^2 \).

Example: \( \frac{3}{1 - 2i} \)

Conjugate of the denominator is \( (1 + 2i) \).

Multiply both by conjugate: \( \frac{3 \times (1 + 2i)}{(1 - 2i)(1 + 2i)} \)

Top: \( 3 + 6i \)

Bottom: \( 1^2 - (2i)^2 = 1 - 4(-1) = 1 + 4 = 5 \)

Answer: \( \frac{3 + 6i}{5} \) or standard form \( \frac{3}{5} + \frac{6}{5}i \)

🎯 Practice — Complex Arithmetic

  1. (Multiplication) Multiply and simplify: \( (3 + 2i)(1 - 4i) \)
  2. (Error Analysis) A student writes \( \frac{5}{2i} = \frac{5}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{i} = \frac{5}{2i} \) and gets stuck. Show the correct method using the conjugate, and find the final answer in \( a + bi \) form.
Answers

1. FOIL: \( 3(1) + 3(-4i) + 2i(1) + 2i(-4i) = 3 - 12i + 2i - 8i^2 = 3 - 10i - 8(-1) = 3 - 10i + 8 = 11 - 10i \).

2. Multiply top and bottom by \( -2i \) (conjugate of \( 2i \) is \( -2i \)): \( \frac{5}{2i} \cdot \frac{-2i}{-2i} = \frac{-10i}{-4i^2} = \frac{-10i}{4} = 0 - \frac{5}{2}i \). Or more simply: \( \frac{5}{2i} \cdot \frac{i}{i} = \frac{5i}{2i^2} = \frac{5i}{-2} = -\frac{5}{2}i \).

4. Complex Solutions of Quadratics MCAP Essential

If the quadratic formula yields a negative under the radical, we extract the \( i \) to write the complex solutions.

Key Rule: Complex roots always come in conjugate pairs! If \( 3 + 4i \) is a root, \( 3 - 4i \) must also be a root.
Example: Solve \( x^2 - 4x + 13 = 0 \)

Formula: \( x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \)

\( a=1, b=-4, c=13 \)

Discriminant: \( (-4)^2 - 4(1)(13) = 16 - 52 = -36 \)

Since \( \Delta = -36 \), we have two complex roots.

Substitute: \( x = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{-36}}{2} \)

Simplify radical: \( \sqrt{-36} = \sqrt{36} \times \sqrt{-1} = 6i \)

\( x = \frac{4 \pm 6i}{2} \)

Divide both terms by 2: \( x = 2 \pm 3i \)

Roots: \( \{2 + 3i, 2 - 3i\} \)

🎯 Practice — Complex Solutions of Quadratics

  1. (Formula Solve) Solve \( x^2 + 6x + 13 = 0 \) using the quadratic formula. Write both roots in \( a + bi \) form.
  2. (Conceptual) If one root of a quadratic with real coefficients is \( 4 - 7i \), what must the other root be? Explain why.
Answers

1. \( \Delta = 36 - 52 = -16 \). \( x = \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{-16}}{2} = \frac{-6 \pm 4i}{2} = -3 \pm 2i \). Roots: \( \{-3 + 2i,\; -3 - 2i\} \).

2. The other root must be \( 4 + 7i \). Complex roots of polynomials with real coefficients always come in conjugate pairs.

5. The Complex Number Plane (Argand Diagram) Classroom/MCAP

Because a complex number has two distinct parts (\( a \) and \( b \)), we can't plot it on a standard 1D number line. We need a 2D plane.

  • x-axis: Becomes the Real Axis
  • y-axis: Becomes the Imaginary Axis

To plot \( z = -3 + 2i \), you move left 3 on the real axis, and up 2 on the imaginary axis. It looks exactly like plotting the coordinate point \( (-3, 2) \) on a normal Cartesian graph.

🎯 Practice — Complex Plane

  1. (Plotting) Plot these complex numbers on the Argand diagram and state which quadrant each is in:
    a) \( z_1 = 2 + 5i \)
    b) \( z_2 = -4 - i \)
    c) \( z_3 = 3i \)
Answers

a) \( (2, 5) \) → Quadrant I (positive real, positive imaginary).

b) \( (-4, -1) \) → Quadrant III (negative real, negative imaginary).

c) \( (0, 3) \) → On the imaginary axis (not in any quadrant). It’s a purely imaginary number.

6. Formative Check & Practice MCAP Essential

Practice:

  1. Simplify: \( (5 - 3i) + (2 + 7i) \)
  2. Multiply: \( (4 - i)^2 \)
  3. Divide: \( \frac{2 + i}{3 + i} \)
  4. Solve for x using the quadratic formula: \( 2x^2 + 2x + 5 = 0 \)
  5. Evaluate: \( i^{51} \)
  6. (Error Analysis) A student simplifies \( \sqrt{-25} \times \sqrt{-4} \) as \( \sqrt{100} = 10 \). What is the correct answer?
  7. (Multi-Part) Given \( z = 3 - 5i \):
    a) Find \( \bar{z} \) (the conjugate)
    b) Compute \( z \cdot \bar{z} \)
    c) What do you notice about the result?
  8. (Reverse Engineering) Write a quadratic equation with real coefficients whose roots are \( 1 + 2i \) and \( 1 - 2i \).
Solutions (Expand to check)

1. Addition: \( (5+2) + (-3i+7i) = 7 + 4i \)

2. Multiplication: \( (4-i)(4-i) = 16 - 4i - 4i + i^2 = 16 - 8i - 1 = 15 - 8i \)

3. Division: Multiply top/bottom by conjugate \( (3-i) \).
Top: \( (2+i)(3-i) = 6 - 2i + 3i - i^2 = 6 + i + 1 = 7 + i \)
Bottom: \( (3+i)(3-i) = 9 - i^2 = 9 + 1 = 10 \)
Answer: \( \frac{7}{10} + \frac{1}{10}i \)

4. Formula: \( \Delta = 2^2 - 4(2)(5) = 4 - 40 = -36 \).
\( x = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{-36}}{4} = \frac{-2 \pm 6i}{4} = \frac{-1 \pm 3i}{2} \)

5. Power of i: \( 51 \div 4 = 12 \) remainder \( 3 \). \( i^{51} = i^3 = -i \).

6. WRONG! You cannot combine square roots of negatives. Correct: \( \sqrt{-25} = 5i \) and \( \sqrt{-4} = 2i \). So \( 5i \times 2i = 10i^2 = 10(-1) = -10 \). Answer: \( -10 \).

7a. \( \bar{z} = 3 + 5i \). 7b. \( (3-5i)(3+5i) = 9 + 25 = 34 \). 7c. The result is always a real number (\( a^2 + b^2 \)). Multiplying by the conjugate eliminates the imaginary part.

8. Factors: \( (x - (1+2i))(x - (1-2i)) = ((x-1) - 2i)((x-1) + 2i) = (x-1)^2 + 4 = x^2 - 2x + 5 \).
