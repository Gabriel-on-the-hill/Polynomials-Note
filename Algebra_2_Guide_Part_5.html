Algebra 2 Excellence Bible — Part 5: Statistics (Elevated)

Core principle: statistics quantifies uncertainty — description first, inference next.

Statistics doesn't remove ambiguity — it measures and explains it.

Overview — Descriptive vs Inferential

Spiral link: Algebra & Geometry (averages, area, ratio). Start here: description (what data says), then inference (what sample says about population).

Tier 1 — Quick Reference (Key Definitions)

  • Population — every unit you care about.
  • Sample — the subset you actually measure.
  • Parameter — number describing population (μ, σ).
  • Statistic — number computed from sample (x̄, s).
  • Descriptive — summarize data (mean, median, IQR, SD).
  • Inferential — use sample to estimate population (CI, hypothesis test).

Descriptive Statistics — Center & Spread

Tier 1 — Essentials

Mean (x̄): arithmetic average. Sensitive to outliers.

Median: middle value. Robust to outliers.

Mode: most frequent value (useful for categorical).

Variance / SD: average squared deviation; SD = √variance.

IQR: Q3 − Q1. Use IQR for skewed data.

Tier 2 — Quick Formulas

Measure Formula Notes
Mean x̄ = (Σx) / n Use for symmetric distributions
Sample variance s² = Σ(x − x̄)² / (n − 1) Unbiased estimator
Standard deviation s = √(s²) Same units as data
Standard error SE = s / √n Estimating variability of x̄

Tier 3 — Robustness & Outliers

When distribution is skewed, prefer median and IQR. For heavy tails, consider trimming or winsorizing. Always show both center and spread.

The Normal Distribution & Z-Scores

Spiral link: Central Limit Theorem — sample means tend toward Normal as n grows.

Tier 1 — 68-95-99.7 Rule

Approximately 68% of observations lie within ±1σ, 95% within ±2σ, 99.7% within ±3σ.

Tier 3 — Central Limit Theorem (Short)

For independent samples with finite variance, the distribution of the sample mean approaches Normal as n increases (n ≥ ~30 is common rule-of-thumb).

Confidence Intervals & Margin of Error

Spiral link: Literal Equations — rearrange formula for unknowns.

Tier 1 — CI (Known σ)

When σ known: CI = x̄ ± z* (σ / √n). For 95% use z* ≈ 1.96.

Tier 2 — MOE & Sample Size (Approx)

Margin of Error (approx): MOE = z* (σ / √n)

To achieve MOE ≤ m: n ≥ (z* σ / m)²

Tier 3 — When σ unknown

For small samples or unknown σ, replace z* with t* (Student's t). Degrees of freedom = n − 1. Use t-table or calculator.

Sampling, Bias & Valid Inference

Spiral link: Logic & probability — inference only valid when sampling is fair.

Tier 1 — Common Bias Types

  • Selection bias: sample systematically excludes groups.
  • Non-response: missing data is not random.
  • Measurement error: faulty instruments or ambiguous questions.

Tier 2 — Practical Checks

  1. Who was included? Who was left out?
  2. How were data collected? Was it voluntary or randomized?
  3. Is the measurement consistent across the sample?

Tier 3 — Reporting & Transparency

Always report sample size, method of sampling, any exclusions, and confidence level. These details determine whether inference is trustworthy.

Common Pitfalls & Practice

Tier 3 — Pitfalls

  • Confusing correlation with causation.
  • Ignoring outliers or masking them with mean alone.
  • Over-interpreting small sample results.
  • Selective reporting (p-hacking) — report full analysis plan.

Practice

  1. Compute mean, median, SD, IQR for a small dataset (5–10 pts) and compare conclusions.
  2. Convert raw score to Z, then compute probability of a more extreme score (two-tailed).
  3. Given x̄=85, σ=6, n=36, compute 95% CI and interpret in plain English.
Worked example — CI

Given x̄=85, σ=6, n=36, z*=1.96: SE = 6/√36 = 1. CI = 85 ± 1.96(1) = [83.04, 86.96]. Interpretation: We are 95% confident the population mean lies in this interval.

Quick Reference & Resources

  • Z critical values: 90%→1.645, 95%→1.96, 99%→2.575.
  • Rule of thumb: n ≥ 30 for CLT; for skewed data, larger n may be needed.
  • When in doubt: visualize — histograms and boxplots reveal structure far faster than numbers alone.
